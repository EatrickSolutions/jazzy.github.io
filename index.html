My Story

The journey of a young leader making a difference

Jascinta Nakivumbu

Miss Uganda High School Contestant | 17

The Leader

Jascinta Nakivumbu is a 17 year old Year 11 student at Empower International Academy, where she proudly serves as the School Vice President. A natural leader with a strong voice and a compassionate heart, she believes leadership is not about position, but about purpose.

The Fashionista

With a deep love for modelling and fashion, Jascinta enjoys transforming style into confidence. Whether she is behind the camera or stepping forward with grace, she uses fashion as a language of self-expression and empowerment.

The Achiever

Beyond the runway, Jascinta is a curious and vibrant young woman. She enjoys debate, reading, playing card games, working out, and learning new things, always seeking growth in both mind and body. Her confidence and eloquence recently earned her the Best Speaker Award at the Humorous Speech Contest.

The Advocate

Driven by a passion for mental health advocacy, Jascinta founded "Sweat to Sparkle", a school based initiative designed to help young girls build confidence, release stress, and refresh their minds through physical activity. She is also committed to breaking menstrual stigma, championing open and inclusive conversations that involve both girls and boys.

My Mission & Passion

My passion for mental health advocacy stems from a desire to empower young individuals to realize their full potential and live fulfilling lives. I believe that every person deserves to feel valued, respected and confident in their worth. Through this platform, I aim to inspire hope, promote positivity and provide support to those navigating life's challenges.

By working together, we can create a culture of empathy, understanding and resilience. My goal is to help young people recognize their strengths, build self belief and cultivate a growth mindset. I hope to contribute to a society where mental health is priotized and individuals feel empowered to speak openly without fear of stigma or judgement.

From Ashes to Beauty

By Jascinta Nakivumbu
January 02, 2026
The first time I visited a Beauty for Ashes workshop, I met Sarah. At 16, she had lost both parents in an accident and was struggling with depression that made it hard to even get out of bed each morning. Through our writing circle, she began to share her pain in small pieces. First just a sentence. Then a paragraph. Then a whole page. Today, Sarah leads our youth writing group and is applying to university to study psychology. She says writing gave her a voice when she felt completely silenced by grief. This is the power of creative expression - it doesn't erase the pain, but it gives us a way to carry it forward into something beautiful.

The Day Hope Returned

By David M.
November 19, 2025
After the landslide that destroyed our home and took three neighbors, I thought I'd never smile again. The grief was a physical weight that made every breath feel heavy. When the Beauty for Ashes team came to our camp, they didn't just bring food and blankets. They brought journals and pens, and they sat with us and listened. At first, I just drew angry scribbles. Then I wrote about the anger. Then about the loss. Then about the memories I wanted to keep. It didn't fix everything. Our home is still gone. But writing gave me a place to put the pain so it wasn't choking me anymore. It made space for hope to return, one word at a time.

Finding Light Again

By Grace L.
November 28, 2025
**Week 1:** The darkness feels permanent. Every morning is a battle just to open my eyes. The grief counsellor says to write, but the words won't come. Just tears on the page. **Week 3:** Today I wrote three sentences about my brother. Not about losing him, but about his laugh. How it sounded like sunshine. My first memory that didn't hurt. **Week 6:** Shared in the Beauty for Ashes writing circle today. Everyone listened without judgement. Someone said my words about sunshine helped them remember light too. Maybe my pain can become someone else's comfort. **Week 10:** I'm writing not just to remember him, but to honor the love we shared. The grief is still here, but there's space around it now. Space for breath. For other memories. For hope.
